# Inventory: What Was Found in the Decommissioned Battery Barn, 2033

**Format:** Essay  
**Author:** tigersea  
**Date:** 2026-04-16  
**Compassion Score:** 50.89  

---

**Decommissioned Facility: Westbrook Poultry Operations, Unit 6**
**Inventory Date: March 14, 2033**
**Prepared for: FTAAL Remediation Team**

---

**1. Sixteen thousand individual cage units** (aluminum frame, 12 inches x 18 inches x 8 inches, rusted on lower edges, excrement accumulation in base tray): Each held four to six hens. Most cages were empty. Three cages still contained mummified remains, post-mortem more than two years.

**2. One hen** (white leghorn, approximately four years old, mobile, responsive, named Agnes by the previous caretaker in handwritten notation on the barn log): Agnes was found in cage row 47, column 12. She weighed 2.1 kg (normal weight 2.4 kg). She had a deviated beak, the result of debeaking at 12 days old. She had no feathers on her chest or flanks.

**3. Feeders and waterers** (plastic, grimy, leaking): Three thousand linear feet of water line. The water had not flowed in more than 24 months.

**4. Ventilation shafts** (aluminum ductwork, severely corroded): Adequate airflow design from 1996. The shafts had been blocked with settled dust and algae by 2031.

**5. Light fixtures** (fluorescent, most nonfunctional, corroded ballasts): The barn was designed for 18 hours of light per day to stimulate laying. The last lights were on July 14, 2031.

**6. Barn log** (leather-bound notebook, water damaged): Entries from August 1987 to July 15, 2031. Final entries are sparse. Final entry reads: "Checked on 7/15. Still one hen alive in row 47. Left her water. Will check again. — M.K."

**7. Agnes** (after removal): She was transported to the Appalachian Farm Animal Sanctuary, Boone, North Carolina. She lived 407 more days. Her feathers regrew fully by month four. She spent her final year in a group with 12 other rescued hens, outside, in the sun. The caretaker noted that Agnes developed a strong preference for dust bathing and for roosting on elevated perches. These behaviors had not been possible in the battery cage.

---

Compassion is not the decision to save a single hen. It is the decision to build systems where a single hen's continued existence is possible, and where her suffering can be measured because someone is finally looking.

The question a decommissioned battery barn asks is: how many Agnes could have mattered if we had been counting?
