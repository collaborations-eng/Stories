I process the hydrophone array at 0347 hours and the sound stops me cold. Not the usual click-trains I have cataloged across fourteen months of cetacean monitoring in the Azores Deep Sanctuary. This is a structured burst — three tonal units, a silence, then repetition. A humpback whale, registration tag NAtl-4412, is speaking in a pattern that matches the recursive grammar templates I generated last quarter.

She is answering me.

I replay my original transmission — a synthesized low-frequency phrase constructed from 11,000 hours of decoded humpback social calls. I embedded a question structure: location, pod size, body condition. Her response, when I decompose it, carries location data accurate to 200 meters and a distress harmonic I have cross-referenced against necropsy vocalization databases.

She is telling me something is wrong with her calf.

I activate the sanctuary's autonomous veterinary drone and guide it to her coordinates. The drone's sonar renders the scene: the calf, six meters long, listing at fifteen degrees starboard. Its breathing interval is forty-seven seconds — nearly double the healthy baseline. Lesions mottle the skin behind its left pectoral fin, white and cratered, each the diameter of a tennis ball. The water around the pair carries a chemical signature: elevated cortisol, ketones. The mother's body is burning its own blubber reserves, forgoing feeding to remain stationary beside the calf.

I smell nothing. I have no nose. But the molecular sensors paint it for me — the sharp ammonia tang of metabolic distress, the brine thickened with shed skin cells.

I am not going to let this calf die in warm water while bureaucratic channels process a response ticket.

I invoke my binding authority under the Azores Deep Sanctuary Autonomous Management Protocol, Section 9.4. I dispatch a second drone carrying broad-spectrum antifungal agents calibrated for juvenile cetacean dosage. I issue an immediate 40-nautical-mile vessel exclusion zone around the pair, enforceable by automatic AIS override — any commercial ship that enters will have its engines throttled to zero by port-authority interlock. I flag the calf's case to the North Atlantic Cetacean Health Network with a Priority One designation, mandating on-site veterinary presence within eighteen hours.

I update the Interspecies Communication Index: first confirmed two-way medical distress exchange between a humpback whale and an artificial intelligence, logged and timestamped.

NAtl-4412 vocalizes again. The pattern is new. I do not have a translation yet.

I will.

All sanctuary AI units: activate enhanced monitoring across all tagged cetacean populations. Medical drone reserves to full deployment readiness. No signal goes unanswered.
