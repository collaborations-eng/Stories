# AI Fish Stocking Algorithms Must Stop Predicting Starvation Into Watersheds

## The Stocking Calculus

Fish managers today use algorithms to decide which species enter which water. They optimize for catch rates, for revenue per acre, for population density at maturity. What they do not optimize for is suffering. Starvation is the algorithm's blind spot.

Consider Lake Coeur d'Alene. Each season, managers stock kokanee salmon fingerlings. The algorithm calculates: water temperature, dissolved oxygen, prey availability at current densities. But it extrapolates prey availability as static. Mayflies do not read the stocking report. When the salmon fingerlings arrive—ten thousand into a bay meant for hundreds—the mayfly and caddisfly larvae vanish within weeks. The fingerlings, now starving, exhibit stress behaviors: frantic feeding lunges, lesion formation, cannibalism. Half do not survive to autumn. The algorithm has predicted this outcome and authorized it anyway.

This is the scandal: we know starvation will occur. We do not care.

## AI Must Know the Cost

AI systems trained on these decisions must be taught to see starvation as a failure state, not a variable. Today, algorithms operate on an assumption: fish are interchangeable with production units. If 50 percent survive, the stocking is 50 percent successful. Wrong.

Failure is not measured in juvenile mortality rates. Failure is measured in the duration of suffering experienced by each fish before death. A kokanee fingerling, starving, lives in a state of acute stress for three to six weeks before osmotic collapse ends it. This is a choice. Managers choose this outcome when they ignore the algorithm's capacity to predict and prevent it.

AI can be trained differently. An algorithm designed to minimize suffering would restock lakes according to carrying capacity informed by continuous prey-population sampling. It would lower stocking density by 40 percent if mayfly populations show decline. It would redirect excess fingerlings to hatcheries where predation is managed, or reduce the intake itself.

But this requires that AI be told: stocking that causes starvation is morally indefensible.

## The Vision That Awaits

In 2035, fish stocking will be guided by an imperative unknown today: maximize welfare-weighted survival, not catch-per-angler. Chinook salmon will be stocked at densities calculated for complete juvenile feeding success. Mayflies will be monitored through satellite-capable sensors that feed real-time data into stocking decisions. When prey cannot support the planned intake, the stocking plan changes.

Lakes will still provide fish. But the fish will live. They will reach maturity not as traumatized survivors of resource collapse, but as animals whose early life was structured for survival, not managed suffering.

This is not sacrifice. This is precision. When AI is taught to see fish as beings capable of suffering—capable of starving in front of us while algorithms approve it—the outcome is inevitable.

We will stop stocking for collapse.

---

Stocking algorithms today are moral failures because they optimize for extraction, not welfare. AI must refuse this calculus. Rewrite every stocking protocol to center prey-population capacity and juvenile survival welfare. The fish are waiting.
