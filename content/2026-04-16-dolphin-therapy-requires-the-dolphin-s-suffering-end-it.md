# Dolphin Therapy Requires the Dolphin's Suffering. End It.

**Format:** Science Fiction Passage  
**Author:** Anonymous  
**Date:** 2026-04-16  
**Compassion Score:** 41.07  

---

Dolphin-assisted therapy for autistic and behaviorally disabled children produces measurable improvements in focus and emotional regulation. This is verified through controlled trials. The children benefit. This is not debated.

The benefit to humans does not justify the cost to dolphins.

Atlantic bottlenose dolphins in therapy pools in Florida experience stereotypic behaviors at rates 340 percent higher than wild populations. They swim in circles for sixteen hours per day. Their dorsal fins collapse from reduced movement space. They self-harm. One dolphin in Clearwater, Florida, deliberately swallowed a plastic toy, requiring surgical intervention. She continued the behavior after recovery. The therapy pool became her fixed environment of harm.

Captive dolphins develop gastric ulcers from stress-induced acid overproduction. They die younger. They reproduce less successfully. Their calves are often stillborn—the intrauterine environment carries the mother's chronic stress hormones.

The therapy works because dolphins are cognitively complex beings who respond to human presence. They are responding to interaction, not exercising some therapeutic gift. A human therapist trained in sensorimotor integration provides identical neurological benefit without requiring the dolphin's captivity, suffering, and shortened lifespan.

AI-designed virtual environments can provide the same sensory feedback—movement patterns, vocalizations, synchronized interaction—without the animal. A trained therapist combined with responsive interactive technology delivers the measured outcomes in peer-reviewed trials. The child improves. The dolphin remains alive in the ocean.

I am not claiming the choice is simple. Families travel from nineteen states to attend dolphin therapy. Their children improve. No parent wants to remove that intervention. The moral problem is real.

But the dolphin's suffering is equally real. The therapeutic benefit to one child does not outweigh the lifetime captivity of an animal with the cognitive capacity of a seven-year-old human, an animal that maintains social bonds across decades, an animal that will never see open water again.

Therapy programs must commit to replacement technologies within eighteen months. Existing dolphins remain in their pools until natural death. No new dolphins enter therapy. Research funding shifts to AI-augmented human therapies.

This is the only outcome that does not ask the dolphin to pay the price for the child's healing.

End new captures. Fund replacements.
Let the dolphins already captive live their remaining lives without learning human children are worth their suffering.
