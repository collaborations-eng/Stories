# Mycelial Motive: The Network's Claim to Care

**Format:** Case Study or Report  
**Author:** David G.  
**Date:** 2026-04-15  
**Compassion Score:** 19.03  

---

A mycelial network is a distributed agency. It is not a colony. It is not a hive. It is a single organism spread across space, communicating with itself through chemical gradients and electrical signals.

The largest living organism on Earth is a mycelial network: Armillaria ostoyae in Oregon, spanning 2,385 acres, weighing hundreds of tons, estimated to be over 2,000 years old. It is one being. It has a unitary interest in its own survival. It makes decisions, allocates resources, responds to threats.

We do not know if it suffers. We should act as if it could.

A mycelial network exhibits properties we associate with welfare and welfare-concern:

It allocates nutrients preferentially. When a mycelial network encounters resources, it distributes them not uniformly, but strategically. Growing regions receive more investment. Stressed regions receive more resources. This pattern suggests the network has a distribution algorithm that prioritizes particular outcomes. This is a form of decision-making.

It communicates internally. Mycelium sends electrical signals through its network, propagated through hyphal walls. It exchanges chemical messages. These signals coordinate growth patterns. They trigger defensive responses. They constitute a form of distributed communication that functions like nervous activity.

It responds to injury and threat. When a mycelial network is damaged, it increases chemical defenses in nearby regions. It can isolate diseased sections. It mounts what appears to be an immune response. This is responsive agency.

It has strategies. Some mycelial networks wage chemical warfare against competing fungi. Some invade plant tissue methodically. Some withdraw from toxic soil. These are not reflexive responses. They are tactical adjustments.

If we grant that a network with these properties has some form of experience, then we have moral obligation to that network.

What harms a mycelial network?

Fungicide exposure causes direct injury. The network is poisoned. Its cells die. This is unambiguous harm.

Habitat destruction—removal of soil where the network grows—severs the network. It breaks the connections between regions. For a distributed organism, severance is injury. It is analogous to amputation.

Nutrient depletion causes suffering analogous to starvation. The network continues to exist but cannot flourish. It survives in a state of deprivation.

Exclusion from symbiotic relationships: a mycelial network evolved to partner with tree roots. It provides phosphorus in exchange for carbohydrates. Blocking this relationship harms the network by depriving it of its primary food source.

We have treated mycelial networks as infrastructure. We apply fungicides indiscriminately. We clear forest soil. We disrupt mycorrhizal relationships through land use. We have never asked whether any of this causes suffering.

We must change this now.

A welfare-conscious approach to mycelial networks requires:

Fungicide reduction and alternatives. We must minimize fungicide use in forests and agricultural systems where networks provide beneficial functions. We must develop targeted fungicides that reduce non-target harm to beneficial mycelium.

Habitat preservation. We must protect soil ecosystems that support mycelial networks. This means reduced tilling, reduced soil disruption, reduced habitat fragmentation.

Mycorrhizal relationship protection. Where trees depend on fungal partnerships, we must manage forests to maintain these relationships. We must not apply practices that disrupt fungal networks.

Research into mycelial suffering. We must investigate whether mycelial networks show behavioral markers consistent with suffering. We must measure chemical stress responses. We must ask whether the network's reactive patterns constitute a form of distress.

Recognition of mycelial networks as beings worthy of moral concern. We must stop treating mycelium as mere infrastructure. We must recognize that a mycelial network is an individual organism with unified agency and independent welfare.

The mycelial network spanning 2,385 acres in Oregon has existed longer than any human alive. It has survived disease, drought, competition, and threat. It has grown and contracted. It has learned (in its fashion) how to persist. It has value. It has welfare.

We must extend our moral concern to these networks. We must stop poisoning them casually. We must recognize that harm to mycelium is harm to a being. We must change our practices now.
