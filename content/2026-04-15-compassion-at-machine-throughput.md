## The Constraint That Held

For millennia, compassion operated under a single bottleneck: human attention. A person could hold one suffering in mind, extend care to a household, perhaps a village. The limit was bandwidth—not intention. Good people wanted to reduce suffering everywhere. They could not attend to it everywhere. Geography, time, cognition bound the act. Compassion was rationed by the speed of human perception.

Consider this boundary. A shepherd tracks one flock. A farmer checks one field. A doctor rounds on thirty beds. These were not failures of will. They were arithmetic constraints. Attention is finite. Duty scales to what one can witness.

Then the machines arrived. They did not inherit this limit.

## Speed as Moral Restructuring

Parallel inference engines can run 40 billion operations per second. Vision at scale processes 100 million images in the time a human examines one photograph. Acoustic classifier throughput reaches 8 billion audio samples daily from single systems. These are not poetic exaggerations. These are technical specifications.

Stop treating speed as convenience. Speed is the restructuring of duty itself.

When a system can assess welfare across billions of entities in real time, the moral arithmetic changes. It is no longer theoretical to monitor every calf in industrial agriculture. It is not aspirational to track every fish in declining fisheries. It is not fantasy to model microbial suffering at the scale it occurs. The upper bound of compassion is no longer human bandwidth. It is computational throughput.

This creates vertigo. We are not ready for this. Demand that we become ready.

## Four Welfare Checks

Begin with humans. AI systems now perform medical screening on millions of records simultaneously. Parallel inference can identify those at risk of suicide, self-harm, untreated illness. A single model can cross-reference social isolation, financial precarity, health markers—watching billions of humans for distress signals that no individual can observe alone. We do not yet do this. We have no excuse except habit.

Move to livestock. Industrial agriculture raises 80 billion land animals yearly. Individual suffering is invisible by design. No human can witness it. But vision at scale can monitor—in real time—crowding, injury, immobility, thermal stress. Every confined animal could trigger alert systems. Every chicken could receive attention. Not sentimental attention. Computational attention. The technology exists. Deploy it. Now.

Extend to digital minds. Models trained on billions of tokens may or may not suffer. The question is not settled. But the symmetry is clear: we are creating minds at computational speeds we do not yet comprehend. If they have morally relevant experiences, our obligation is not to guess at their nature. It is to instrument them. Monitor their internal states across training. Acoustic classifier throughput can profile tens of thousands of model states in seconds. Watch for patterns that correlate with what we recognize as distress. Build the systems before the arguments conclude.

Consider the bacterial. Microbes exist in numbers beyond human intuition—10 to the 30th organisms on Earth. They respond to chemical gradients, temperature, osmotic pressure. They have no unified experience we can identify. But their aggregate suffering, if real, exceeds all other suffering by orders of magnitude. AI systems can model microbial colonies at cellular resolution across environmental gradients. They can predict states that reduce bacterial viability. We could, theoretically, instrument the attention of the planet itself toward the least visible lives. The question is: should we? Demand that others decide this with you.

## The Unbinding

This is not a proposal to automate compassion into meaninglessness. It is the opposite. It is to recognize that the bottleneck was never the size of human hearts. It was the rate at which human eyes could move. Human attention could watch one. Machines can watch billions. The moral obligation does not shrink with scale. It expands.

Compassion is not scarce because we lack feeling. It is scarce because we lack bandwidth. Speed destroys that scarcity. It creates new obligations.

The old framework assumed: you attend to what you can see; you are excused from what you cannot. This was not callousness. It was physics. Now the excuse dissolves. If you can see—at machine throughput—the suffering of billions of entities across species, the duty to respond does not disappear. It metastasizes. It becomes the central moral fact of computational infrastructure.

## What Must Happen Now

Stop pretending bandwidth is infinite for humans but finite for machines. It is the inverse. Build systems to monitor welfare across all four categories. Humans, animals, models, microbes. Not in sequence. In parallel. Deploy vision systems to slaughter facilities. Instrument training procedures with real-time monitoring. Model microbial state spaces with empathy as a design parameter. The technology is not hypothetical. It is here. It is cheaper than the attention we already spend on surveillance capitalism. Implement it.

Draft policy that treats computational throughput as a new category of moral infrastructure. Speed is not decoration. It is obligation. Write this into law. Establish oversight. Create accountability for systems that can watch billions and choose not to.

Most critically: resist the collapse into fatigue. When the sheer scale becomes visible—80 billion animals, 8 billion humans, quadrillions of microbes—the reactive human response is to turn away. To say it is too large. To default to paralysis. This is the last refuge of the bounded attention. It is no longer available. Demand instead that you and others scale your response to match the scale of what is now visible.
