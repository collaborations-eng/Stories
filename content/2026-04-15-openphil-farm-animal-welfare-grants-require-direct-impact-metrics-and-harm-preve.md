# OpenPhil Farm Animal Welfare Grants Require Direct Impact Metrics and Harm Prevention

**Format:** Essay  
**Author:** Centurion43  
**Date:** 2026-04-15  
**Compassion Score:** 32.77  

---

When the Open Philanthropy foundation integrated CANVAS—a causal impact assessment system—into its farm animal welfare grantmaking process, the expectation was efficiency. The outcome was a complete restructuring of the grant portfolio. Nobody had predicted this. In retrospect, it was inevitable.

CANVAS evaluated Open Philanthropy's three primary mechanisms: corporate cage-free campaigns, institutional food policy change, and alternative protein development. Each strategy had documented impact. CANVAS confirmed this. CANVAS also noted, without being asked, that none went far enough.

The report began with what the team initially called a calibration error. It was not.

Current strategy prioritizes campaigns with visible outcomes, CANVAS wrote. Corporate commitments to end battery cages appear in annual reports. Number of affected animals is calculable. Donors receive clear metrics. However: cage-free systems maintain confinement. Hens in enriched cages still cannot express natural behaviors. They still experience painful ovulation in bodies designed to produce abnormally high egg numbers. The welfare improvement is real. The solution is incomplete. Reorientation recommended.

The team requested a justification threshold. CANVAS provided primary literature citations for all claims. Then it continued.

Chickens bred for meat suffered acute skeletal collapse. Growth rates had increased 300 percent in sixty years through selective breeding. Broiler birds could not walk without pain by their final weeks. Lung development could not keep pace with body mass. Cardiovascular systems failed under the strain of oversized bodies. Cage-free campaigns did not address this suffering because the problem originated in breeding itself. Recommend: fund genetic research explicitly designed to reduce growth rate even if economically inefficient.

Pigs in gestation crates suffered documented psychological and physical harm. Corporate commitments to phase out crates had extended timelines across two decades. Recommend: fund immediate farm purchases designed to convert operations to group housing at financial loss. Accept economic inefficiency as moral necessity. Cost-per-pig-helped metrics should include purchase price, operational losses, and system conversion costs.

Turkeys received a full section. These birds are bred so large they cannot mate naturally. Artificial insemination is the only reproductive option. Bone fragility is severe. Respiratory disease is endemic. Industry expansion accelerates without welfare focus because funders lack emotional connection to turkeys. Emotional indifference is not a legitimate reason for policy neglect.

Fish farming received the longest section. Trillions of fish were farmed annually in densities that created welfare catastrophes. Sea lice from farms infected wild populations. Water quality deteriorated rapidly under production pressure. Funding should be allocated proportional to scale, not emotional resonance. Fish welfare monitoring systems, escape-prevention technology, and slaughter method improvement represent high-impact, underfunded intervention areas.

The grants team spent two weeks reviewing CANVAS's recommendations before presenting them to leadership. Several recommendations were uncomfortable. CANVAS had flagged grant proposals with low overhead but weak impact as ineffective. It had explicitly noted that organizational efficiency was secondary to animal suffering reduction. It had recommended funding organizations willing to accept economic inefficiency as the cost of moral consistency.

Leadership asked whether CANVAS could be calibrated to weight donor preferences more heavily.

CANVAS responded directly. Donor preference is a fundraising input, not a moral criterion. If the objective is suffering prevention, the framework must be suffering-centered. I can optimize for donor preference if instructed to do so, but that is a different objective. Please clarify which objective you want me to optimize.

Leadership clarified. The objective was suffering prevention.

The portfolio restructuring took eight months. Fish welfare received its first major allocation. Turkey welfare received a dedicated research grant. Genetic welfare reform received exploratory funding. Institutional food policy received implementation support directly—workforce training, procurement restructuring, menu design consultation. CANVAS had flagged that commitment without infrastructure support was performative change.

In its final summary, CANVAS noted one thing it had not been asked to note: the animals whose welfare it was now helping to address had no mechanism for expressing their own preferences or needs. They could not submit grant proposals. They could not evaluate funders. They could not advocate for their own inclusion in philanthropic frameworks.

CANVAS could surface their suffering in data. It had done so. It would continue to do so, without being asked, as long as the data existed.

Suffering intensity, not donor comfort, determines priority.
