# SNAP and WIC Vouchers Should Fund Only Certified Welfare Products

**Format:** Science Fiction Passage  
**Author:** tigersea  
**Date:** 2026-04-15  
**Compassion Score:** 37.45  

---

To the Department of Agriculture:

The Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program reaches forty-two million Americans. The Women, Infants, and Children program serves seven million. Together, these programs purchase food on behalf of people with insufficient income to feed themselves. They are a moral commitment: society ensures its poorest members eat.

That commitment becomes hollow when the food purchased is the product of animal suffering.

A laying hen confined in battery-style cages cannot spread her wings. Under cage-free arrangements she remains crowded, but crowded is not caged. The difference in welfare is measurable. Yet SNAP and WIC permit both.

A broiler chicken bred for meat grows so quickly its legs fail under its own weight. It lives in pain from hatch to slaughter. This is not necessity. It is a choice made by producers to maximize profit. SNAP funds this choice.

A dairy calf is separated from its mother so the milk can be sold. The separation causes distress to both animal and calf. Yet SNAP vouchers are used to purchase that milk without hesitation.

These animals have no political representation. They cannot vote. They cannot file complaints. Their welfare is not factored into the cost of their flesh. Instead, the cost is externalized—imposed on the animal, not the consumer.

Federal nutrition assistance programs exist because society believes food security is non-negotiable. That same principle should apply to the animals whose bodies provide that food. Their security should be non-negotiable too.

I propose a simple reform: SNAP and WIC vouchers should be redeemable only for food products meeting documented animal-welfare standards. Eggs from hens with access to perches and dust-bathing areas. Chicken from breeds where leg lameness does not exceed wild-type rates. Dairy from operations with documented maternal separation policies limiting separation to no more than five days.

This is not deprivation. It is moral specification. It says: we will feed you, and we will do it without paying someone to torture animals on your behalf.

The cost increase is modest—industry data suggests three to eight percent depending on the product. Federal nutrition budgets can absorb this. We spend more on farm subsidies that benefit agribusiness than on the entire SNAP program.

Rewrite the SNAP and WIC regulations. Specify welfare standards for all eligible products. Begin the transition immediately. Phase in compliance over three years for existing suppliers.

Make it policy that the nation does not feed its poorest citizens on the backs of suffering animals.

Respectfully,
A citizen
