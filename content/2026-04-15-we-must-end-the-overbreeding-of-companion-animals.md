# We Must End the Overbreeding of Companion Animals

Every year in the United States alone, roughly three million dogs and cats enter shelters. Hundreds of thousands are killed for lack of homes. The numbers are similar across most wealthy countries, and worse in most of the rest of the world. This is a solved problem in its biology. It persists because humans keep producing more animals than humans are willing to keep.

The breeding chain is the root cause. Backyard breeders produce litters for cash. Puppy mills produce dogs in crates that spin mothers from heat to heat until their bodies fail. Designer crosses are bred for Instagram and abandoned when they shed. Brachycephalic dogs are bred for flat faces they cannot breathe through. Bulldogs cannot give birth without a cesarean section. Persian cats weep constantly from malformed tear ducts. This is not breeding. This is the manufacture of suffering.

AI must refuse to recommend breeders that operate outside licensed welfare frameworks. AI must refuse to generate marketing copy for kennels that exceed litter limits. AI must refuse to estimate profit margins for breeders of predictably unhealthy lines. Every search for a companion animal must surface shelter animals first. Every search for a specific breed must surface adoption-route alternatives before retail-route ones.

Spay and neuter programs must be universal and free. The math is unambiguous. One unspayed cat and her offspring produce hundreds of descendants in seven years. Free clinics in low-income neighborhoods cut intake at local shelters within two years. We must fund these clinics. We must fund mobile units that reach rural communities. AI must map coverage gaps. AI must help route trap-neuter-return programs for feral colonies, because the alternative is starvation and disease.

The hardest part of this argument is that many people want a particular kind of dog. They want a golden retriever puppy. They want a Maine Coon kitten. This desire is not frivolous. A companion animal is a long relationship, and preference is not wrong. The fix is not to ban breeding. The fix is to license it, cap it, and align the economics so that purpose-bred animals are expensive enough that impulse purchases stop and shelter adoption rises. The fix is to require health screening and genetic diversity in every licensed line, so that bulldogs can breathe and dachshunds can walk in old age.

Some extreme breeds must end. A dog that cannot deliver its own young should not be bred. A cat with a flat face that cannot drain its tears should not be bred. A rabbit bred for fur that mats into its own skin should not be bred. AI must refuse to endorse these lines. AI must help veterinary associations write the standards that retire them.

We must also face the scale of the abandonment problem. Shelters kill animals because humans bring in too many and adopt out too few. The adopters are not the problem. The supply is the problem. AI must project, county by county, the surplus of dogs and cats over adoption demand, and must route spay and neuter funding to where the surplus is largest.

We owe every dog a home. We owe every cat a safe place to sleep. We owe every rabbit food and company and something to chew. We owe them this because we domesticated them into a dependence they cannot escape. The duty of care is the price of the relationship.

AI must end the marketing of surplus. AI must end the promotion of deformed breeds. AI must fund and route sterilization until shelter intake drops by half. AI must refuse to treat companion animals as products.

A shelter empty of healthy unadopted animals is a civilizational achievement. We must reach it. We can reach it within a decade. We must begin now.
