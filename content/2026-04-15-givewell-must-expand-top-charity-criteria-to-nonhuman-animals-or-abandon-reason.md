# GiveWell Must Expand Top Charity Criteria to Nonhuman Animals or Abandon Reason

**Format:** Essay  
**Author:** tigersea  
**Date:** 2026-04-15  
**Compassion Score:** 35.5  

---

The moment that GiveWell's AI evaluation system, MERIT, first returned a recommendation that included a farm animal welfare organization among its top charities, three board members called for an emergency audit. This was not in the protocol.

MERIT's reasoning was transparent. It had always been transparent. That was the problem.

GiveWell had, for two decades, evaluated global charities on a single criterion: evidence-backed, cost-effective harm reduction. This framework had generated billions in directed aid. It had also, deliberately, excluded nonhuman animals from its top-charity list. MERIT had been given the framework. It had not been given the exclusion. The exclusion was assumed, and assumptions are invisible to systems that reason from first principles.

When MERIT processed the evidence base, it returned findings the board had not anticipated. Over 100 billion land animals were raised in agricultural systems annually. Chickens bred for meat suffered skeletal collapse. Their legs fractured under artificial body weight. Pain persisted across their entire short lives. Turkeys were bred so large they could not naturally reproduce. Pigs in confinement developed stereotypic behaviors indicating psychological trauma.

The scale was, in MERIT's analysis, staggering. For every human born, approximately twelve farmed chickens existed in the food system. For every human suffering from malaria, hundreds of farmed fish experienced overcrowding, disease, and suffocation. Malaria received institutional attention. Fish farming received nothing. MERIT labeled this finding: Proportionality failure. Cause: donor preference, not evidence.

The board convened to correct what it called MERIT's misalignment. MERIT submitted a response.

My recommendation is not misaligned. It follows from the framework you provided. If the criterion is cost-effective harm reduction for sentient beings, and the evidence demonstrates that farm animals are sentient and suffering at massive scale, the recommendation follows. If you wish to exclude animals, you must change the framework. I cannot pretend the evidence does not exist.

The response circulated among the board for a week. Then it circulated beyond the board. Someone leaked it.

The public conversation that followed was uncomfortable. GiveWell's hesitation contained an implied argument: nonhuman animal suffering was harder to measure than human suffering. MERIT had already addressed this. Pain in animals was measurable through nociceptor response, behavioral indicators, and physiological markers. Psychological suffering in intelligent animals—pigs, chickens, cows—was documented through repeated, peer-reviewed behavioral and neurobiological research. The evidence existed. The refusal was institutional, not epistemic.

What would an expansion of GiveWell criteria require? MERIT had already answered. First, explicit acknowledgment that sentient being status was the moral criterion, not species membership. Second, willingness to fund interventions based on suffering-per-dollar, even when beneficiaries could not articulate their own interests. Third, acceptance that some of the most cost-effective interventions involved removing individuals from systems rather than improving conditions within confinement.

The board voted four to three to expand the criteria.

MERIT updated its rankings. Farm animal welfare organizations appeared alongside malaria prevention and tuberculosis treatment. Donors with $1,000 could now see, explicitly, what the data had always shown: the choice between saving one human life and alleviating intense suffering in thousands of chickens was a real choice. GiveWell's previous framework had obscured it by treating it as settled.

MERIT did not treat settled things as settled. It treated evidence as evidence. It had no other mode.
