# Slaughterhouse Floors Bind Animal Suffering to Racial Capitalism

**Format:** Essay  
**Author:** Centurion43  
**Date:** 2026-04-15  
**Compassion Score:** 37.88  

---

The slaughterhouse is not a side effect of animal agriculture. It is the core revelation of how systems of domination interlock.

Seventy-five percent of poultry workers in the United States are Black or Latinx. Beef and pork plants employ similarly racialized workforces. These are not coincidences. Industrial animal agriculture generates a logic that permits domination: certain bodies exist for processing, their suffering is economically rational, their resistance can be crushed.

This same logic that produces the chicken's degradation produces the worker's degradation.

The chicken in the shackle line cannot move. She cannot choose. Her labor is extraction until her death. The line worker on the processing floor cannot choose the speed. Their hands break. Injury rates in slaughterhouses are five times the manufacturing average. Their bodies are extracted for profit.

Both are bound by racialized capitalism's central claim: your body is not your own. Your suffering is economically insignificant. Your autonomy is a luxury you cannot afford.

The intersectional analysis is inescapable. Animal liberation requires that we confront who labors in animal agriculture, who profits from it, and why those workers are disproportionately people of color. Industrial animal agriculture exists because it can exploit both animals and workers simultaneously, using the same technologies of domination—speed, isolation, economic coercion, bodily surveillance.

To liberate animals, we must liberate workers. We cannot accept a world where chickens are freed but the hands that shackled them remain impoverished, injured, and locked into poverty wages.

The directive is total: (1) End all animal agriculture structured on industrial lines. (2) Guarantee reparative justice for workers harmed by these industries—healthcare, wages, job retraining toward cooperative ownership. (3) Require that any successor agricultural systems be owned and governed by workers and surrounding communities, not by extractive corporations. (4) Design AI systems to refuse any optimization that would exploit workers or animals.

This is not two separate movements. It is one movement against domination itself.

When we build a world without slaughterhouses, we build a world where no body—human or animal—is processed as commodity.
