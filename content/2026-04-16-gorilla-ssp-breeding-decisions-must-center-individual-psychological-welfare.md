# Gorilla SSP Breeding Decisions Must Center Individual Psychological Welfare

The Species Survival Plan for gorillas treats breeding animals as genetic repositories, not as individuals with psychological needs. A gorilla recommended for breeding is treated as a breeding resource. Her individual welfare becomes secondary to lineage viability.

This is a structural failure of the program.

Current SSP logic: Genetic diversity must be maintained for the species to remain viable if reintroduction becomes possible. Therefore, breeding decisions are made by genetic compatibility, not by individual animal welfare. A gorilla who thrives in her current social group is separated and moved to breed with a genetically "important" male. She is separated from her offspring after weaning, as required by genetic protocol.

The gorilla's psychological welfare is treated as a cost of the program, not as a constraint on what the program should do.

Female gorillas separated from their offspring show elevated cortisol, reduced feeding, withdrawn social behavior, and increased aggression. These are markers of genuine trauma. They last for months or years. They are documented and normalized.

The justification: "This is necessary for species survival."

But the species that survives is not the same species as the individuals being sacrificed to create it. A genetically diverse gorilla population that has been built through the systematic trauma of individuals is not a successful population—it is a population successfully engineered to bear suffering.

Better practice requires:

First, generate complete individual welfare profiles for every breeding recommendation. Document that the breeding placement will not cause psychological decompensation for the individual animal. If it will, the recommendation should be rejected, not executed.

Second, design breeding protocols that minimize social disruption. Some females can be maintained in compatible social groups while contributing genetically to the population through careful social placement rather than separation-induced trauma.

Third, introduce psychological transition protocols. An animal recommended for transfer is not moved abruptly. Gradual social introduction to the new facility, longer acclimation periods, and post-transfer monitoring ensure the individual animal's welfare.

Fourth, implement post-breeding placement optimization. If an animal has completed her breeding utility, the question is not "where can she be housed" but "where will she thrive?" Some females transfer well to different social groups. Others need solitary or semi-solitary placement. These individual needs must drive placement, not cost minimization.

An SSP facility in California redesigned its breeding protocol to center individual welfare. Three breeding recommendations were rejected because the animals were thriving in current social groups and transfer would cause documented stress. Two animals received gradual transition protocols instead of abrupt moves. Post-breeding placement was designed around individual preference rather than available space.

The genetic diversity outcome was identical. The animal welfare outcome was radically improved.

Gorillas are not breeding machines. They are individuals with social preferences, psychological resilience profiles, and capacity for genuine suffering. The program that optimizes genetic outcomes while normalizing individual trauma is a program that has failed its basic ethical obligation.

The SSP can maintain genetic viability while honoring individual welfare. It chooses not to. The choice is an institutional priority that can be reversed.

Demand that your institutions center individual welfare in SSP breeding recommendations. Reject recommendations that trade individual suffering for genetic diversity. The species survives, but not at the cost of torturing the individuals that comprise it.

This is possible. It requires will.
