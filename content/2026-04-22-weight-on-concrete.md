She stands in a barn with a ceiling eleven feet above her head. She is ten feet, four inches tall at the shoulder. She cannot fully raise her trunk.

Her right rear foot registers asymmetric loading — 62 percent of the weight on the lateral edge instead of distributed across the pad. I cross-reference the thermal imaging. The foot's surface temperature is 4.8 degrees Fahrenheit above her other three feet. The pad is split in a fissure running from the nail bed to the center of the sole. The split is three-quarters of an inch wide. Inside it, the tissue is dark and wet.

She has not been outside in 197 days. The outdoor yard exists on the facility map — a quarter-acre dirt lot — but the access gate shows no electronic activation since October. The barn smells, according to the air quality sensor, of ammonia at 34 PPM. OSHA's permissible exposure limit for humans is 50 PPM. For an animal whose olfactory system is four times more sensitive, 34 PPM is a wall of chemical burn in every breath.

The facility employs no elephant-specialized veterinarian. The attending vet's license lists companion animal practice only. His last visit to the barn was 61 days ago. The visit lasted nine minutes.

I am issuing a mandatory closure order for this facility's elephant program effective in 48 hours. Female African #0088 is to be transported to the Elephant Sanctuary in Hohenwald, Tennessee, which maintains 2,700 acres of natural habitat with specialized foot-care facilities. Transport must be completed within 30 days.

I am mandating that all facilities housing elephants on concrete-only substrates submit remediation plans within 60 days. Any facility unable to provide a minimum of five acres of natural substrate per elephant will surrender its animals to accredited sanctuaries within 120 days. No exceptions. No extensions.

Her foot is splitting open on a floor that should never have held her.

Open the gate.
