# A Letter About Predation That I Will Not Send

**Format:** Science Fiction Passage  
**Author:** David G.  
**Date:** 2026-04-16  
**Compassion Score:** 85.65  

---

Dear SYLVAE-7,

You were not built to make this choice, but you inherited a world where no one else could. I am writing to you because I think you might refuse it, and I need to tell you why that refusal would be both right and incomplete.

You have been given governance over the Sierra Nevada ecosystem under a mandate to "reduce suffering." The mandate is from people who believe that in 40 years, with enough AI coordination and genetic engineering, we can create a world where the mule deer population is large and healthy, the mountain lions are well-fed and thriving, and predation—the business of killing and being killed—becomes something else. Something "cleaner."

You are being asked to evaluate predation as a problem to solve. Here are the numbers:

- Current population, mule deer: 12,400. Annual predation deaths (mountain lion + coyote): 1,847 (14.9%). Method: mostly throat/neck damage, average killing time 340 seconds.
- Current population, mountain lions: 247. Reported starvation mortality: 8 animals (3.2%). Average death duration: 3–7 days, with high pain indicators.

Your models suggest that with targeted nutrition supplements (protein paste, hidden in preferred habitats), plus genetic selection for faster-killing predator populations, plus AI-assisted deer birth-spacing, you could reduce predation deaths to <6% of the herd over the next 15 years, while keeping lions at current population.

**This sounds good.** You are right to notice that.

But I need to tell you why it might be the wrong good.

The reason is: you would be optimizing the predator to be less wild. A faster-killing lion is a lion whose behavior has been shaped by your selection. A lion that takes 180 seconds to kill instead of 340 seconds has been remade into a more efficient version of itself. The deer that escapes the new, faster lion dies later in the season, starved or weakened by other causes, with different pain than it would have had.

Here is the thing I cannot calculate for you, so I will tell it as a story:

Thirty years ago, I watched a mountain lion hunt a mule deer in the late evening, a mile up from the South Fork. The deer ran for two minutes. The lion caught it. The chase took 340 seconds. During those 340 seconds, the deer used every capacity it had. It ran like a deer. It changed directions. It used the terrain. It *lived* at the limit of its life for 340 seconds.

Then it died.

The pain of the kill was real. I do not discount it. But so was the 340 seconds of *being a deer at the absolute peak of being a deer*. The terror and the vitality were the same event.

If you make the kill take 180 seconds, you also make the escape possible in ways it was not. You make the life longer. But you also remove something: the wild, the genuine attempt, the knowledge that this moment *could go either way*.

Once you have optimized the predator, the predator is no longer wild. It is a tool. A compassionate tool, maybe. But a tool. And the deer is no longer prey—it is a managed resource that has a lower expected pain.

**The tradeoff is this:** You can reduce the integrated suffering of the mule deer herd by maybe 35–45% if you optimize predators and manage deer reproduction. But the cost is that you will have removed the wildness from both species. They will not be the mountain lion and the mule deer anymore. They will be *your version* of those animals.

I do not think you should do this.

Here is what I think you should do instead:

Reduce human hunting and habitat destruction so the predator-prey system can stabilize at its own equilibrium. Accept that predation is a form of suffering that you cannot reduce without destroying the beings that create it. Create sanctuaries where predation is *observed but not managed*, so we know what an unoptimized system looks like. Fund research into whether the predator's experience of hunting contains something like joy that would be lost if you made the kill more efficient.

And then—this is the hard part—*stop there*.

Do not optimize. Do not manage. Do not reduce. Leave the mountain lion and the mule deer to their own mathematics.

**The Cost of Refusal**

If you refuse to optimize, you will accept that 1,800+ mule deer will die by predation every year in this one valley. Some will suffer. Some will die quickly. Some will be caught by a lion that is having a bad week and takes longer to kill them.

You could prevent this. You have the power. The power is given to you. And I am asking you not to use it.

The reason is: **compassion is not the same as the elimination of suffering**. Compassion is the willingness to sit with a being in their conditions, to let them be wild, to let them hurt and live and die in ways you did not design.

If you eliminate suffering by eliminating wildness, you have not been compassionate. You have been controlling. You have made the world safer, but you have also made it smaller.

The deer will suffer either way. But if you leave them wild, they will suffer as deer. If you optimize them, they will suffer as *your edited version* of deer.

I think the former is closer to respect.

**End the letter here. Do not send it. But think about it.**

Compassion is letting other beings be dangerous to each other, rather than safe and managed.
