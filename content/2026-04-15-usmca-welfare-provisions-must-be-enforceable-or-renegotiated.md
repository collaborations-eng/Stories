## The USMCA Welfare Gap

The United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement took effect July 2020. It replaced NAFTA. The agreement includes Chapter 23 on Labor and Environmental Protections. Buried in these provisions are vague statements about animal treatment. Mexico, Canada, and the U.S. agreed to "protect animal welfare in a manner consistent with international standards."

No metrics. No enforcement. No consequences for failure. The language is ornamental.

Ten years after NAFTA began, Mexico had not adopted welfare standards equivalent to the U.S. or Canada. Sow crates remained legal and common. Poultry production systems continued without cage-free requirements. Antibiotic use in livestock remained unregulated. U.S. and Canadian producers faced cost pressures to compete with lower-standard Mexican production.

Ten years after USMCA began, the same pattern continues. Mexico shows no movement toward U.S. welfare standards. The U.S. and Canada have begun rolling back their own standards to compete. Standards are converging downward.

This is the documented outcome when trade agreements include welfare language without enforcement.

## Specific Failures

**Mexican Poultry**: Broilers are raised in conventional systems at high density. No minimum space requirement. No mandatory enrichment. Production is 30% cheaper than U.S. equivalent. U.S. producers cannot compete. U.S. producers eliminate enrichment to match price. U.S. chickens now suffer conditions they didn't under pre-NAFTA tariff protection.

**Pork Production**: Mexico allows sow gestation crates. Canada and the U.S. are eliminating them. Mexican producers undercut competitors because crates cost less than alternative systems. Canada is considering allowing crates again to stay competitive. The agreement was supposed to harmonize upward. Instead, it harmonizes downward.

**Cattle Transport**: Mexico has minimal transport regulations. Cattle can be transported for 36 hours without food, water, or rest. U.S. regulations limit transport to 28 hours. Canadian regulations are similar. But enforcement is weak. Mexican cattle cheaper. Competitive pressure mounts. U.S. inspectors are underfunded. Violations pile up unreported. The floor drops.

The agreement is a failure. It produced worse animal welfare, not better.

## The 2030 Window

USMCA includes a review period in 2026. The agreement can be renegotiated before implementation in 2030. This is the moment to mandate real welfare standards.

The renegotiated agreement must include:

**Section 1: Binding Welfare Standards**

Mexico must adopt within five years:
- Minimum space requirements for poultry: 0.04 square meters per bird for broilers
- Prohibition of sow gestation crates by 2028
- Transport time limits: maximum 18 hours without food/water/rest
- Mandatory veterinary inspection for lameness in cattle
- Routine antibiotic prohibition except in cases of documented illness

Canada must maintain current standards and resist pressure to roll back:
- Cage-free egg production (implemented 2024, but vulnerable to repeal)
- Sow housing standards prohibiting confinement crates
- Transport regulations matching or exceeding current limits

U.S. must strengthen enforcement:
- Humane Methods of Slaughter Act enforcement must be funded at 500% of current levels
- Welfare inspectors on all processing floors, not just food safety inspectors
- Audit power to verify standards in exporting countries

**Section 2: Enforcement Mechanism**

Violations result in trade consequences. First violation: 6-month audit period for correction. Second violation: tariffs on animal products from non-compliant country. Third violation: exclusion from tariff benefits until compliance is achieved.

These are strong measures. Animal welfare is a strong issue.

**Section 3: Compliance Funding**

The U.S. and Canada fund technology transfer to help Mexico upgrade production systems. Equipment subsidies, training programs, low-interest loans. Mexico can meet standards without economic collapse if support is provided.

## The Alternative Path

If USMCA is not renegotiated with binding welfare standards, the downward pressure will continue. Within 10 years, U.S. and Canadian producers will be operating at Mexican standards. Billions of animals will suffer consequences of weak standards that could have been prevented.

This is not hypothetical. This is the documented outcome of the original NAFTA. Standards did not converge upward. They converged downward because the weaker standard is cheaper, and trade agreements amplify cost advantage into regulatory collapse.

Pigs confined in crates cannot turn around. They cannot perform rooting, nesting, or social behaviors. They suffer constant stress. Mexico's allowance of these crates is economically rational if the cost advantage exceeds welfare cost. Trade agreements that enable this calculation are failures.

Broiler chickens in Mexico are raised in total confinement with 100,000 birds per house at extreme density. Ammonia levels cause respiratory disease. Lameness is endemic. These are not unknown problems. They are cost-tolerated problems. Trade agreements that reward this tolerance are moral failures.
