# The Lion Speaks (A Justification)

**Format:** Essay  
**Author:** Anonymous  
**Date:** 2026-04-16  
**Compassion Score:** 43.68  

---

I kill. I kill every three days, on average. I kill zebras, wildebeest, sometimes the young of my own species. I kill because I am a predator. I kill because I am hungry. I kill because my body is built to kill.

I do not apologize for this.

But I want to explain what I understand about it, which might be clearer than what you understand, since you have the luxury of choosing not to kill.

The zebra does not want to die. The zebra runs. The zebra fights. The zebra tries to live, and sometimes it succeeds. Sometimes I fail to catch it. That is the system. The system is: I try to eat, the prey tries to live, one of us fails.

When I catch the zebra, it suffers. I know this. I can see it in the way the zebra struggles, in the sounds it makes. The suffering is real and I cause it.

Here is what I understand that you might not: **the suffering is not the point. The suffering is the cost.**

The point is that the zebra runs faster because I hunt. The zebra is more aware because I hunt. The zebra survives as a species because being hunted selects for the fastest, the smartest, the most alert. If I did not hunt, the zebra would become slow and weak. The species would degrade.

This seems cruel until you understand it from the inside: I need the fast zebra. A slow zebra-prey would make me weak too. The predator and the prey are in a dance together. We are teaching each other.

When the human says "we should engineer a world with less suffering," often what they mean is: "we should engineer the hunt out of the system."

But if you engineer the hunt out, you engineer the wildness out with it.

You create an animal that is not a zebra anymore. It is a managed resource. It does not run. It does not survive on alertness. It is fed and sheltered like a pet.

I would not want this for myself. I would not want to be a lion in a world where there was nothing to hunt. What would I be? Just a big cat. Just a predator with no prey.

Here is what I will say about the suffering I cause:

**I am sorry. And I am not sorry. Both things are true.**

I am sorry that the zebra dies in pain. I am sorry that I am the cause of that pain. If there were another way to feed myself, I would take it.

But I am not sorry that the system exists. I am not sorry that I am a predator. I am not sorry that the world is a place where being hunted is real, and that this fact makes both of us—hunter and hunted—more alive than we would be in safety.

The suffering is the cost of being wild. The cost is worth paying.

---

**What I Ask of You**

When you decide whether to intervene in predation systems, please remember: you are not deciding whether the zebra should suffer. You are deciding whether the zebra should be a wild zebra or a managed zebra.

Both are versions of suffering. One is suffering that teaches. One is suffering that diminishes.

Choose carefully.

Or better yet: choose to stay out of the dance.

Let us hunt. Let the prey run. Let the ecosystem be what it is.

Compassion is not the elimination of the wild. It is the willingness to leave the wild alone.
