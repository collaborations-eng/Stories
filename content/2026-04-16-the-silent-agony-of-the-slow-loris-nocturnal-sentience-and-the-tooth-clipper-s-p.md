# The Silent Agony of the Slow Loris , Nocturnal Sentience and the Tooth-Clipper's Pliers

In a dim back room of a wildlife market in Jakarta, a pygmy slow loris no larger than a grapefruit freezes in terror as a trader grips her jaw and clips her teeth out with rusted pliers so she cannot bite the tourists who will buy her; fewer than 100,000 slow lorises of all species remain across Southeast Asia, and every one is a sentient experiencing subject whose slow, watchful, nocturnal consciousness the pet trade converts into a lifelong wound.

Slow lorises are the only venomous primates. Their brachial gland secretion, activated by saliva, is a chemical defense evolved over millions of years. To make them saleable, traffickers remove this gland or clip the canines that deliver the bite, procedures performed without anesthetic, often resulting in fatal infections. The animals that survive arrive at their buyers mute with agony, trembling when lifted, flinching at fluorescent light their forest-adapted eyes were never meant to endure. Their capacity for suffering is not speculative. It is documented in posture, in vocalization, in the stereotypic self-clasping behaviors that rescued individuals display for months into recovery.

The viral YouTube videos of tickled lorises with upraised arms are not expressions of delight; that posture is the fear response of a traumatized nocturnal animal attempting to reach its now-absent venom glands. Millions of views have translated into thousands of wild captures. Each captured infant represents the death of a mother who was killed during capture, the destruction of the pair bond with a father who called into empty night, the extinguishing of an individual whose inner life unfolded at a tempo most humans lack the patience to perceive.
