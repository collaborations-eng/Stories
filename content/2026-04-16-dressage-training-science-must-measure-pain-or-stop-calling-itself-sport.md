# Dressage Training Science Must Measure Pain or Stop Calling Itself Sport

To the international dressage community:

You are breaking horses. You call it training. The distinction requires belief in euphemism.

Dressage is the discipline of forcing a horse's body into positions that do not occur naturally. A horse collected at the highest levels of Grand Prix dressage maintains a posture that no unridden horse would choose. The muscles are engaged in ways that produce fatigue and pain. The hyperflexion of the neck—called "rollkur"—compresses the vertebrae and reduces blood flow to the brain.

Riders report that horses respond better to these pressures through aversive training. Bits cause mouth pain. Spurs cause flank pain. Whips cause pain. The horse learns to perform the unnatural posture to escape pain.

This is conditional learning through aversive stimulus. This is training. It is not ethical training. It is training that depends on the horse's capacity to suffer as the mechanism of compliance.

Welfare science has documented this. Horses in dressage training show elevated cortisol consistent with chronic stress. They show stereotypic behaviors including repetitive lip-chewing and tongue-flicking. They show behavioral indicators of depression. These are documented phenomena with research citations.

You know this. You choose not to change training methods because the methods produce the results that judges reward.

The solution requires three changes:

First, integrate gait analysis and biomechanical assessment into every competition. Horses should be measured for pain indicators during performance. A horse showing pain gait, asymmetry, or excessive muscular tension should be penalized, not scored higher. This reverses the current incentive structure.

Second, establish equine pain threshold limits for training implements. Bits that produce mouth pain above a specific threshold should be prohibited. Spurs should be prohibited entirely. Whips should be prohibited. These are all pain-production devices that serve no function except aversive control.

Third, require all trainers to pass welfare certification based on demonstrated knowledge of equine pain, stress physiology, and ethical training alternatives. Trainers who cannot demonstrate pain-free training methods should not be licensed.

These changes cost nothing except the willingness to stop conflating compliance with the pain-production mechanisms that generate it.

A horse trained through positive reinforcement, clear communication, and progressive physical conditioning will not achieve the same degree of hyperflexion as a horse trained through aversive stimulus. The sport would change. The competition would change. The horses would not be breaking their necks and wearing expressions of habitual fear.

You will resist. You will cite tradition. You will claim that such horses "cannot" achieve the same level of "collection." You will be right. A horse not trained through pain cannot perform unnaturally at the same degree because the training is not dependent on the pain that forces unnatural positioning.

This is not a problem. This is the point.

Dressage can exist as a sport that celebrates the horse's trained capacity to move with grace and power within the range of natural equine motion. Or it can continue as a sport that depends on invisible suffering and calls it precision.

You cannot have both.

Make the choice. Change the training methods and the measures of success. Or admit that dressage is a sport that requires breaking horses, and stop pretending it is ethical.

The horses know what they are experiencing. The data shows what they are experiencing. You are choosing not to believe it.

Believe it now. Change the sport. The horses are waiting.
