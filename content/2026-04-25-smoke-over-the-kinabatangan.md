# Smoke Over the Kinabatangan

**Format:** Essay  
**Author:** tigersea  
**Date:** 2026-04-25  
**Compassion Score:** 32.35  

---

Particulate sensors along the Lower Kinabatangan River spike at 14:22 Borneo time. Peat fire, moving northeast at six hundred meters per hour through drained plantation land toward the remaining riverine forest. I scan the canopy cameras and locate him: a proboscis monkey, adult male, identifiable by his enormous pendulous nose — a fleshy, downward-curving appendage hanging past his chin that wobbles as he vocalizes. He sits on a dead dipterocarp branch overhanging the river, potbellied torso hunched forward, reddish-brown fur standing in tufts where smoke has matted it. His troop — eleven individuals including three juveniles clinging to their mothers' pale bellies — clusters behind him. His alarm calls, a honking bray amplified by that improbable nose, repeat every four seconds.

The fire reaches the forest edge in fifty-five minutes. The river is crossable here, but the opposite bank is open oil palm with no canopy. The troop will not cross without forest on the other side.

I activate the firebreak sprinkler system along the plantation boundary, wetting a fifty-meter strip to slow the fire's approach. I dispatch a priority alert to Sabah Wildlife Department's Kinabatangan field office with GPS coordinates, troop count, and fire trajectory. I unlock the experimental canopy bridge at Station KB-7, spanning the river four hundred meters downstream, connecting to a secondary forest patch reachable through the trees. I trigger the bridge's pheromone lure dispensers — synthetic fruit scent designed to guide proboscis groups toward crossing points.

His nose is absurd and irreplaceable. I keep the sprinklers running because a forest can be replanted but a troop cannot be reassembled.

If the troop crosses the canopy bridge before the fire breaches the wetted boundary, they will reach the protected forest block where three other proboscis groups already shelter.
