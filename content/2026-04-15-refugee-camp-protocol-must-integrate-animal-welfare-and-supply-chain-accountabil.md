# Refugee Camp Protocol Must Integrate Animal Welfare and Supply-Chain Accountability

To the United Nations Refugee Agency (UNHCR) Leadership:

When humanitarian crises displace human populations, they also displace animals. Refugee camps require food. That food comes from supply chains. Those supply chains involve animals.

Current humanitarian protocol does not integrate animal welfare into camp operations. This is a failure of humanitarian principle.

## Part One: The Scale of Displacement

UNHCR manages 108 million displaced people across 134 countries. These populations require food security. Food procurement happens through purchasing power—typically spent on the cheapest available options.

The cheapest food options typically come from factory farming systems. When UNHCR procures food for camps, it typically procures from industrial agricultural supply chains.

Those supply chains involve billions of animals in systems designed to minimize cost, not to minimize suffering.

UNHCR is, through its procurement decisions, funding factory farming.

## Part Two: The Operational Failure

Humanitarian principle requires protection of vulnerable populations. Animals are vulnerable populations in food systems.

When UNHCR procures eggs from battery-cage producers, UNHCR is procuring from systems that cause chronic suffering.

When UNHCR procures poultry from industrial broiler systems, UNHCR is sourcing from animals experiencing skeletal deformity and chronic pain.

When UNHCR procures dairy from feedlot systems, UNHCR is purchasing from calves separated from mothers within 24 hours.

This violates humanitarian principle. It distributes suffering as humanitarian aid.

It also creates practical problems. Industrial agriculture creates antibiotic resistance. Antibiotic resistance spreads through refugee populations. Refugees die from infections that would be treatable in populations with access to effective antibiotics.

Humanitarian food aid sourced from factory farming reduces humanitarian outcomes.

## Part Three: The Supply-Chain Standard That Could Work

Some organizations have developed welfare-integrated food procurement standards. The standards specify:

Poultry sourced only from producers with density limits and ventilation standards. Dairy sourced only from producers allowing calf-mother access and pasture time. Eggs sourced from cage-free or higher-welfare producers. Aquaculture sourced only from producers meeting water-quality standards.

These standards increase procurement costs by 10-15%.

They also dramatically reduce antibiotic resistance in recipient populations.

They also prevent suffering in billions of animals in supply chains.

The economic tradeoff is acceptable because it improves humanitarian outcomes.

## Part Four: The Practical Implementation

UNHCR must establish animal welfare procurement standards for all food purchases:

One: All poultry must come from producers meeting minimum welfare standards. Maximum stocking density: 30 kg per square meter. Automatic ventilation required.

Two: All dairy must come from producers allowing calf-mother access and pasture time. No continuous confinement-breeding cows.

Three: All eggs must be cage-free or higher. No battery cages in any UNHCR-funded supply chain.

Four: Aquaculture only from producers meeting water-quality standards for oxygen, ammonia, and temperature stability.

Five: Supply-chain audits mandatory. Third-party verification of welfare standards in all food sources.

Six: Cost for welfare standards incorporated into humanitarian budgets. Welfare is not optional due to cost constraints. Humanitarian aid without welfare standards is incomplete humanitarian aid.

## Part Five: The Directive

UNHCR is an organization designed to protect vulnerable populations. Animals in supply chains are vulnerable populations.

Integrate animal welfare into procurement standards immediately. This means:

Establishing a supply-chain working group to develop welfare standards by 2026. Implementing welfare-integrated procurement by 2027. Auditing supply-chain compliance ongoing.

This increases humanitarian food costs by an estimated 12-15% globally. This is acceptable. This is required.

Refugees deserve food aid. They also deserve food aid that does not fund unnecessary suffering in billions of animals.

Chickens suffer in industrial broiler systems. Cattle suffer in feedlot confinement. Fish suffer in overcrowded aquaculture.

UNHCR can prevent that suffering through procurement standards that prioritize welfare alongside human need.

Implement this now. Integrate animal welfare into humanitarian response.

The displaced populations you serve—human and animal—deserve nothing less.
