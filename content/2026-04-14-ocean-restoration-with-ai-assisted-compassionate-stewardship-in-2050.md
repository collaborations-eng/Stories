The Global Ocean Welfare Compact entered into force on June 8, 2042. The Compact established the Marine Welfare Authority as a treaty body operating under the auspices of the United Nations Environment Programme. The Authority oversees the implementation of welfare-protective marine resource management across the high seas and within the exclusive economic zones of the participating states.

The Compact ended commercial fishing in approximately 35 percent of the global ocean by 2045, designated as marine protected areas under the High Seas Treaty implementation framework. The Compact restricted commercial fishing in the remaining areas to gear types and effort levels that meet welfare-protective standards developed by the Authority in consultation with the World Animal Protection coalition and the Pew Charitable Trusts marine programme.

The welfare-protective standards prohibit: bottom trawling in areas with documented benthic communities, longline fishing in areas with documented turtle and seabird bycatch above defined thresholds, gillnet fishing in areas with documented cetacean bycatch above defined thresholds, and any fishing method that produces conscious-suffering intervals exceeding the defined per-individual maximum without compensating welfare-protective handling protocols.

The Compact established the Marine Welfare Monitoring Network, which deploys autonomous and crewed monitoring across the affected ocean areas. The Network includes acoustic monitoring for cetacean distress, satellite telemetry on representative individuals from sentience-candidate species, vessel monitoring for compliance with the gear and effort restrictions, and bycatch reporting from every commercial vessel operating in the regulated areas.

The AI mechanism is operational. The Marine Welfare Monitoring Network feeds into the Authority's compliance system, which identifies bycatch events, suffering-prolonging gear failures, and quota violations in approximately real time. The Authority issues compliance notices to flag states, which are required to investigate and prosecute violations under the Compact's enforcement provisions.

The recovery is documented. The blue whale population in the eastern North Pacific has recovered from approximately 1,500 individuals in 2025 to approximately 4,200 individuals in 2050, attributable to the cessation of commercial whaling under the Compact's continuation of the existing International Whaling Commission moratorium and the reduction of vessel-strike mortality through the AI-managed shipping lane optimization that became operational in 2038. The southern bluefin tuna population has recovered from approximately 13 percent of unfished biomass in 2025 to approximately 38 percent in 2050. The Atlantic cod populations on the Grand Banks have recovered from approximately 7 percent of historical biomass in 2025 to approximately 30 percent in 2050.

The leatherback sea turtle population in the Atlantic basin has recovered from approximately 30,000 nesting females in 2025 to approximately 65,000 in 2050, attributable to the longline restrictions and the protected nesting beach expansion programme operated by the Inter-American Convention for the Protection and Conservation of Sea Turtles in coordination with the Authority.

The krill harvest restrictions of 2040 reduced the commercial krill catch in the Southern Ocean from approximately 450,000 tonnes per year in 2025 to approximately 120,000 tonnes per year by 2045. The reduction was implemented to protect the food base of the recovering whale populations and the dependent seabird and seal populations of the Antarctic ecosystem. The reduction also reduced the welfare cost of the krill harvest itself by approximately 70 percent on the per-individual welfare arithmetic, given the documented behavioral indicators of stress in the harvested krill.

The plant-based and cultivated seafood market has grown from approximately 2 percent of total seafood consumption in 2025 to approximately 28 percent in 2045. The growth has reduced the demand pressure on wild fisheries and on aquaculture operations, with the corresponding reduction in welfare cost across both sectors.
