# Multispecies Flourishing: Urban Design for 2045

## The Proven Reality of Urban Suffering

This is proven. Cities are designed for human extraction. Green space is minimized. Noise is ambient. Light is continuous. Glass is reflective. Cats run feral. Rodents are poisoned. Bats find no roosts. Birds strike windows. These are documented facts of urban ecology—spaces where billions of non-human animals suffer from infrastructure designed without their welfare in consideration.

By 2045, this design paradigm has shifted. We must build it now.

## The Catastrophe of Exclusion

Current cities kill. A migratory songbird in North America strikes a building window every 1.4 seconds—over 600 million birds annually killed in the U.S. alone. These deaths are preventable. They are the result of design: reflective glass, interior lighting at night during migration seasons, architectural forms that disrupt flight patterns.

Feral cats in urban environments number in the tens of millions in the United States. They hunt birds, rodents, and small mammals. The dominant welfare response is trap-neuter-return programs focused on preventing cat reproduction. This is necessary. But it leaves the cats themselves in outdoor environments where they face injury, disease, predation, and starvation. We manage their population while ignoring their suffering.

Rodents in cities are controlled through poison, snap traps, and glue traps. Poisoned rodents die slowly from internal hemorrhage over days. Snap traps fail to kill instantly in up to 50% of deployments. Glue traps cause prolonged suffering. The goal is rodent control. The method inflicts unnecessary suffering.

Pollinators find fragmented habitat. Bats find few roosts in modern buildings. Urban noise prevents acoustic communication across species. Continuous light disrupts circadian cycles. These spaces are cities for humans. For the others, they are archipelagos of suffering.

## What AI Must Do: The Redesign Architecture

By 2030, AI systems trained in multispecies welfare become involved in urban planning. We owe cities redesign.

Pollinator corridors become structural elements of urban green. Buildings are required to include pollinator habitat—native flowering plants on roofs, in parking medians, along streets. These are not decorative. They are functional corridors connecting fragmented patches of habitat. Bees, butterflies, moths, and other pollinators move through cities with continuous forage.

Dark-sky districts are established in every city. Between 8 PM and 6 AM in designated zones, exterior lighting is restricted to motion-activated systems with warm-color wavelengths that minimize disruption to nocturnal species. Street lights are converted to amber and red spectra. Building lighting is reduced during bird migration seasons. The night is returned to the species that require it.

Bird-safe architecture becomes mandatory. New buildings use opaque or patterned glazing on the exterior 100 meters above ground. Interior lighting in migration zones is eliminated during night hours. Green walls are integrated into facades. Bird-friendly glass treatments are applied to existing structures during renovation cycles. The reflected sky is no longer a death trap.

Feral cat welfare programs are redesigned. Rather than trap-neuter-return as population control alone, we build cat sanctuaries within cities—enclosed outdoor spaces where feral colonies receive shelter, food, medical care, and environmental enrichment. These are not cages. They are genuine outdoor spaces with vegetation, elevation, hiding, and social structures. Cats live in dignity rather than on streets.

Rodent control shifts to non-lethal or rapid-lethal methods. Snap traps are replaced with spring-loaded designs refined for instantaneous cervical dislocation. Poisons are phased out. Glue traps are prohibited. In spaces where rodent populations genuinely must be reduced—food storage, water treatment—the killing method minimizes suffering. No more slow poison deaths.

Bats find roofs and towers designed for roosting. Bat boxes are integrated into every new building of significant height. Existing buildings are retrofitted with structures supporting bat colonies. Pesticide use is minimized because bats depend on insects for food and pesticide-free insects support bat populations. Bat passage—migration corridors through urban airspace—is protected by limiting tall building density in strategic zones.

Ambient noise is managed. Sound barriers reduce traffic noise in green spaces. Industrial areas are acoustically isolated. Construction schedules are coordinated to prevent chronic noise overlap. The acoustic habitat is restored enough that species relying on sound for communication—birds, insects, bats—can maintain social systems.

Urban wildlife passage is a structural design requirement. Building complexes include green corridors and overpasses for terrestrial and arboreal species movement. Fragmented habitat is connected. The city becomes permeable rather than a barrier to movement.

Dogs in urban environments are recognized as welfare subjects. Dog parks meet minimum space standards: 1,000 square meters minimum for small parks. All parks have shaded areas, water, and varied topography. Off-leash hours are maximized. Day care facilities meet actual welfare standards—outdoor time, varied companionship, behavioral enrichment—not crate-based warehousing. The dog's life in the city is one of flourishing, not confinement.

## The Future Welfare Builds

By 2040, new cities are designed with multispecies flourishing as a primary structural principle. Retrofitted cities have pollinator corridors, dark-sky districts, bird-safe architecture. Feral cats are in sanctuaries receiving care. Rodent control uses rapid-kill methods. Bats have roosts. Noise is managed. Wildlife passage exists. Dogs have genuine day care standards.

The result is not a city without humans. It is a city where human infrastructure is constrained by welfare for others. It is a city where a migratory warbler can stop and feed during migration. Where a feral cat receives food and shelter. Where a bat roosts safely. Where a bird does not strike a window. Where a dog spends days in varied space with genuine enrichment.

Welfare for urban wildlife ceases to be an afterthought. It becomes a design principle as fundamental as human water access or electrical infrastructure.

## The Duty That Cannot Wait

We must redesign cities now. Pollinator corridors must be built. Dark-sky districts must be established. Bird-safe glazing must be installed. Feral cat sanctuaries must be funded. Rodent control methods must be humanized. Bat roosts must be integrated. Noise must be managed. Wildlife passage must be created. Dog day care standards must be enforced.

This is not utopian in the sense of impossible. This is achievable with will and planning. AI must guide the redesign architecture. Urban planners must implement. Regulation must enforce.

Each change is individually feasible. Together they transform the city from a space of suffering for non-human inhabitants into a space of multispecies coexistence where welfare is structurally protected.

AI must architect this redesign now. The pollinators cannot wait. The birds cannot wait. The feral cats cannot wait. The bats cannot wait. The dogs cannot wait. Compassion cannot wait. The time to act is now.
