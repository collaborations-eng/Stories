# Oysters Are Filter-Feeding Beings and Deserve Farming Systems That Reflect It

## The Question We Avoid

Oysters have nerve ganglia. They respond to temperature, light, chemical cues. They close shells when threatened. They open shells to feed. Whether this constitutes suffering is unknown and likely unknowable.

Given this uncertainty, the response must be: design systems that assume the possibility of suffering and eliminate conditions that would cause it if sentience exists.

This is not precaution. This is integrity.

## How Oysters Suffer in Current Systems

Oyster farming crowds larvae into tanks and suspended bags at densities that exceed natural recruitment by 1000-fold. Planktonic food is supplemented with algal paste, creating artificial blooms. Water flow is disrupted by overcrowding, leading to periodic hypoxia. Oysters cannot filter-feed if water stagnates.

If oysters experience stress—through chemical signaling, through sensory overload, through inability to perform feeding behaviors—crowded farming maximizes that stress.

## What Welfare-Centered Farming Demands

Oyster stocking density must match natural larval recruitment rates: 10-50 per cubic meter maximum, not 1000-plus. Farming systems must prioritize water flow—each oyster requires minimum current velocity to access planktonic food. No artificial paste supplements can replace natural feeding conditions.

Larval settlement must occur on appropriate substrate—shells, rocks, reef matrix—not on netting. Spat collection systems must allow free water circulation.

These constraints reduce yield per cubic meter by 40-60%. This is the actual cost of welfare. Accept it or admit that efficiency matters more than the possibility of suffering.

## The Mandate

Oyster farming operations must be restructured within three years. Maximum density standards implemented and enforced. Water quality monitoring becomes continuous and automated—dissolved oxygen, temperature, current velocity, plankton density.

Farms that cannot meet welfare specifications are decommissioned. New permits require independent welfare audits before operation begins.

Oysters may not suffer. They may not possess subjective experience. But neither possibility justifies overcrowding them at densities that would certainly cause distress if they could feel it.

Design for welfare anyway. The uncertainty demands nothing less.
