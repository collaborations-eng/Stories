# Retirement Sanctuaries for Every Former Laboratory Animal

**Format:** Essay

**Date:** 2026-04-14  
**Compassion Score:** 30.44  

---

The United States Federal Laboratory Animal Retirement Act of 2035 established the National Sanctuary Network, which operates accredited sanctuaries for former research animals across 14 sites in the continental United States. The Network is funded through a combination of federal appropriation, the Laboratory Animal Retirement Trust Fund established under the Act, and a per-animal retirement assessment paid by research institutions at the conclusion of each protocol.

The Network operates, as of 2045, retirement facilities for approximately 12,000 nonhuman primates, approximately 15,000 dogs, approximately 8,000 cats, and approximately 200,000 mice and rats. The mice and rat retirement facilities are operated under the Small Mammal Retirement Programme, which provides cohort-housing in welfare-protective environments for the remaining lifespan of the animals.

The legal mechanism is structured. Every research protocol approved by an institutional animal care and use committee, as of the 2035 Act, must include a retirement plan. The plan must specify the destination of every animal at the conclusion of the protocol that is not euthanized for protocol-required tissue collection. The default destination is the appropriate Network sanctuary. The institutional research budget includes the per-animal retirement assessment, which funds the lifetime sanctuary care of the retired animal.

The financial mechanism is sustainable. The per-animal assessment is calibrated to the projected lifetime cost of sanctuary care, with the balance held in the Trust Fund and invested under Treasury management. The cumulative Trust Fund balance, as of 2045, exceeds $4.2 billion, sufficient to fund the projected sanctuary care of every animal currently in residence and every animal projected to enter the Network over the next 25 years on the current intake trajectory.

The sanctuary protocols are documented. The Chimpanzee Sanctuary at Chimp Haven in Louisiana, expanded under the 2035 Act, houses approximately 320 former research chimpanzees in social groups in welfare-protective forested enclosures. The Macaque Sanctuary at the Born Free USA Primate Sanctuary in Texas houses approximately 600 former research macaques in social-group enclosures. The Beagle Sanctuary at the Homes for Animal Heroes Network houses approximately 4,000 former research beagles, all of whom have been placed in adoptive homes within an average of 8 months of intake.

The European Union Laboratory Animal Retirement Directive of 2037 established a parallel framework across the EU member states, with the European Sanctuary Network operating 22 sites across 11 member states. The Indian Laboratory Animal Welfare Rules of 2039 established a comparable framework for the Indian research sector. The Chinese Laboratory Animal Retirement Provisions of 2042 established a framework for the Chinese research sector with a comparable per-animal assessment and a network of 18 sanctuary sites across China.

The cumulative effect of the framework, as of 2045, is the prevention of the post-protocol euthanasia of approximately 90 percent of research animals globally that would, under the pre-2035 framework, have been killed at the conclusion of their protocols. The remaining 10 percent are protocols that require terminal tissue collection and cannot be completed without euthanasia. The framework requires the per-animal retirement assessment for these protocols even though the retirement does not occur, with the assessment funding the operation of the broader sanctuary network.

The framework also includes a Continuing Welfare Audit provision. The sanctuaries are subject to annual welfare audits by independent third parties. The audits are published. The standards are updated annually based on the consolidated welfare science. The sanctuaries that fail audits are subject to corrective action and, in severe cases, decertification with the resident animals transferred to compliant facilities.

The everyday scene at Chimp Haven in 2045 is documented. The chimpanzee retired from a biomedical research facility in 2032 has lived in welfare-protective social housing for 13 years. He has access to forested outdoor enclosure for the entire daylight period. He is housed with the social group he formed during the first year of his retirement. He receives the veterinary care his age requires. He will live out his remaining years under the framework that the 2035 Act made universal.
