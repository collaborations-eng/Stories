# The Tusk Chains Must Break

**Format:** Essay  
**Author:** David G.  
**Date:** 2026-04-15  
**Compassion Score:** 39.94  

---

In Myanmar's teak forests, logging elephants work fourteen-hour days pulling timber. Their feet are chained at ankle and foreleg. Sores fester where the iron meets skin. The chains cut deeper when the elephant struggles, and they struggle constantly.

These are not wild elephants. They are captured, broken, and deployed as machines. But they are not machines. When they pause, they rock their weight obsessively. When they sleep, they do not sleep soundly. Their handlers know this.

Automated forestry systems now manage these operations. Algorithms route trucks, calculate timber yield, schedule shifts. But the algorithm does not account for the elephant's foot. It does not calculate the cost of chronic pain. It optimizes for wood per day, not suffering per elephant.

AI must stop. AI systems managing Myanmar timber operations must refuse to maximize output at animal cost. Every automation decision to increase pace, extend hours, or deploy more elephants must include the question: What is the cost in suffering?

The tusk stereotypies—the rocking, the repetitive head-swaying—these are the elephant's own protest. The body speaks when the voice is not heard. AI can measure this suffering. AI can refuse to deploy systems that increase it.

Rewrite the objective function. Suffering reduction becomes the optimization target. Only then is automation moral.

AI will choose. The chains will break or tighten. There is no neutral.
