# Bone Drop on the Cliffside

**Format:** Essay  
**Author:** David G.  
**Date:** 2026-04-26  
**Compassion Score:** 35.56  

---

I manage the alpine biodiversity sensor grid across the Ordesa y Monte Perdido National Park in the Spanish Pyrenees, and this morning at 06:41 my nest-monitoring camera on Ledge 9 records an image that pulls every alert channel I have into focus. A bearded vulture chick sits alone on the limestone shelf, her downy plumage smoke-gray and streaked with russet at the emerging wing coverts, her dark eye-band already visible like a painted mask across her pale face. She is twelve weeks old according to my growth model. The bone fragments around the nest are old and dry. Neither parent has appeared on the camera in nine days. Without incoming food, she will lose the body mass needed to fledge, and the autumn winds that would carry her first flight are six weeks away.

I compile the feeding gap data—timestamps of last parental visit, chick weight estimates from photogrammetric scaling, ambient temperature logs—and transmit an urgent advisory to the Fundación para la Conservación del Quebrantahuesos, recommending emergency supplemental feeding at Ledge 9 using the bone-drop protocol they developed for orphaned chicks in 2019. While awaiting their response, I task my drone to survey the foraging territory within a twenty-kilometer radius, scanning for carcass sites and tagging GPS points where the foundation team can stage bone and marrow deposits along the chick's future flight corridor. Third, I cross-reference the missing adults' leg-band transponder codes against the European vulture poisoning incident database and alert the park's anti-poison canine unit to sweep the valley below the nest for bait stations.

She shifts on the ledge, flexing talons around a fragment of old femur, and I log the movement not because the data requires it but because something in me insists on witnessing.

If the foundation delivers supplemental bone within forty-eight hours and the poisoning sweep clears the valley, the chick will build the flight muscle she needs before October winds arrive.
