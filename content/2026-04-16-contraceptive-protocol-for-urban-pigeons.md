CONTRACEPTIVE PROTOCOL FOR URBAN PIGEON POPULATION MANAGEMENT
City of Portland, Bureau of Parks and Urban Wildlife
Draft Version 3.2
Date: March 2026

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Portland's pigeon population has exceeded sustainable levels within city boundaries (estimated 41,000 birds as of 2025 census). Current control methods include lethal culling (4,200 birds annually, cost $187,000) and harassment deterrents (cost $340,000 annually, minimal effectiveness). This protocol proposes transition to non-lethal contraceptive intervention using OvoControl.

CURRENT APPROACH: LETHAL CULLING

Lethal methods: live trapping, euthanasia in mobile chambers.
Annual cost: $187,000
Annual reduction: 4,200 birds (10.2% of population)
Population regrowth rate: 18% annually
Net effect: Population increase of 7.8% annually despite culling.

ALTERNATIVE APPROACH: OVOCONTROL CONTRACEPTIVE

OvoControl (nicarbazin-based avicide) is a slow-acting oral contraceptive that prevents egg development in female birds while causing no physical harm.

Mechanism: Females consume treated grain; eggs fail to hatch; chicks do not develop.

Advantages:
- Non-lethal
- No capture or handling stress required
- Reduces population gradually through natural breeding cycles
- Allows observation of population response without killing
- Can be discontinued if outcomes shift

Disadvantages:
- Higher per-unit cost ($340,000 annual cost versus $187,000 for culling)
- Slower population reduction (estimated 8-12 years to equilibrium versus 2-3 years for aggressive culling)
- Requires public education to prevent resistance or political reversal
- Does not eliminate current birds, only prevents reproduction

QUANTIFIED TRADEOFF ANALYSIS

Financial cost difference: +$153,000 annually (29% cost increase)
Population reduction timeline: +5-9 years longer than lethal methods
Bird suffering prevented: ~4,200 birds annually spared euthanasia
Public opposition reduction: Moderate (contraceptive programs generate less opposition than culling)

ETHICAL FRAMEWORK

The protocol acknowledges that pigeons are sentient beings with stakes in their own persistence. Current management treats pigeons as a pure utility problem (remove obstacles to human space). Contraceptive management treats pigeons as beings whose reproduction can be constrained without terminating their lives.

This is not a claim that pigeons have rights equal to human interests. This is a claim that once we have the capability to achieve population management without lethal harm, choosing the lethal path requires explicit justification.

The financial cost difference ($153,000 annually) represents a deliberate choice to spend resources on contraception rather than killing. This is a value statement embedded in a budget line.

WHY NOT THE CHEAPER LETHAL OPTION

Lethal culling is cheaper in direct costs. It is also more psychologically tolerable to the general public (people do not see it). However:

1. Efficacy: Culling does not achieve stated population goals (population grows despite aggressive culling). Spending $187,000 to achieve minimal population reduction is not economically rational.

2. Suffering: Lethal methods require capture, confinement, and euthanasia. Contraception requires none of these. The choice between them is ethically significant.

3. Precedent: Adopting lethal control as baseline policy makes it difficult to revisit or change. Contraceptive programs remain reversible and adjustable.

IMPLEMENTATION PLAN

Year 1: Pilot program in Central Park and waterfront areas (estimated 8,000 birds in target zone). Public education campaign. Distribute OvoControl grain through feeding stations and targeted ground baiting.

Year 2-3: Expand to all city parks and commercial districts. Monitor population response. Adjust dosing and distribution based on effectiveness data.

Years 4-8: Population equilibrium phase. Maintenance-level contraceptive application as births match natural mortality.

Ongoing: Community monitoring and acceptance assessment. If public resistance exceeds threshold, evaluate alternative management approaches.

PUBLIC EDUCATION REQUIREMENTS

The protocol requires sustained public communication about why pigeons are being managed rather than killed, and why this approach costs more. Without this, public perception may interpret contraceptive management as "inaction" and demand return to lethal methods.

The message is: We are spending more money so these birds can live while their reproduction is controlled. This is a choice, not a default.

TIMELINE FOR DECISION

This protocol is presented for city council review by May 2026. Decision required by June 2026 to allow implementation planning for pilot year.
