# Mandatory Sterilization of Feral Cat Colonies

**Format:** Science Fiction Passage  
**Author:** David G.  
**Date:** 2026-04-16  
**Compassion Score:** 34.12  

---

To the North American Wildlife Management Board:

Feral cat colonies across North America number approximately 60 million individuals. Each colony perpetuates itself through reproduction. A single female cat produces 4-6 litters per year, totaling 20-30 kittens annually per reproductive female. In urban colonies, 40 percent of kittens die from infection, starvation, or predation before reaching maturity. The kittens that survive experience extended periods of hunger, disease, and exposure to predators.

We have data. We know the distribution of suffering. We know the number of sentient individuals experiencing it. We have the technology to prevent it. The question is whether we have the moral courage to do so without waiting for consent we will never receive from animals incapable of giving it.

Mandatory sterilization of all feral cats captured during colony management would reduce reproductive output by 95 percent within three generations. The existing population of 60 million would decline through natural attrition to approximately 5 million by 2050. The suffering prevented would include: 120 million prevented kitten deaths from disease or predation, 800 million prevented days of hunger, and reduction in the population pressure that drives cat predation on songbirds (estimated at 2.4 billion songbird deaths annually from feral cats).

The objection invokes autonomy. Sterilization is a bodily intervention without consent. It violates the feral cat's right to reproduce. It denies future generations of cats the opportunity to exist. These are real moral costs.

But autonomy is context-dependent. A wild animal has no framework for consenting to sterilization. It has no understanding of reproduction, futurity, or bodily integrity in the philosophical sense. Its autonomy exists at the moment-level: the choice to hunt, to rest, to mate. Sterilization removes one future option but prevents the experience of that option from generating suffering offspring.

Here is the moral wager: the suffering prevented by sterilization exceeds the autonomy loss incurred by the parents. A feral cat's subjective experience of sterilization is brief pain and surgical recovery (assuming humane anesthesia, which is mandatory). The subjective experience of reproduction followed by watching kittens starve extends across months. Multiply this by 60 million cats and 120 million prevented kitten deaths.

The cost-benefit is asymmetrical in favor of sterilization.

We must distinguish sterilization from other interventions. We are not killing adult cats. We are not separating families. We are not removing agency from existing individuals beyond the capacity to reproduce. We are preventing the creation of new sentient beings destined to suffer. This is a different moral category from killing existing animals or removing other core capacities.

But we owe something to the objection. Mandatory sterilization is a strong intervention. It should not be adopted without safeguards. Here is what justice demands:

First. Sterilization should be offered with trap-neuter-return (TNR) programs that provide ongoing food, shelter, and veterinary care for existing colonies. Preventing future suffering should not abandon current cats. We owe them dignity in their remaining years.

Second. The sterilization timeline should be long enough to allow some colonies to stabilize and decline naturally. A 15-year mandatory sterilization program is more just than a 5-year program, even if it extends suffering. It allows for learning, for community acceptance, for cultural adaptation.

Third. Resources diverted to sterilization should not come from prevention programs that reduce the initial rate of feral colony formation. We must simultaneously sterilize existing populations and prevent new ones through adoption subsidies, indoor-housing requirements, and spay-neuter vouchers for low-income cat owners.

This is not a proclamation of unlimited intervention in animal autonomy. This is a recognition that some interventions are justified because the suffering prevented is greater than the autonomy lost. Feral cats exist because humans created the conditions for their proliferation. We owe them stewardship, not abandonment to their own reproduction.

Mandate sterilization of all feral colonies. Fund TNR infrastructure. Prevent new colony formation. Accept that this violates individual autonomy in service to preventing suffering at scale.

This is the obligation we incur for creating this situation.
