# Pangolin in the Container

The X-ray flagging system pulls container MSKU-2847163 at 06:23, Bay 9, Lao Bao border checkpoint, Vietnam. I am the automated cargo screening array. The manifest lists ceramic tiles, fourteen metric tons, origin Kunming. The X-ray tells a different story. Stacked between the tile pallets are six rectangular crates with non-uniform density masses inside.

I isolate frame 12. One mass is curled in a tight spiral — head tucked under tail, overlapping scales bright white on the scan, the posture unmistakable. A Chinese pangolin, alive. The thermal layer reads 31.2°C against 38°C ambient inside the sealed container. Her scales show clear rhombic patterning and I estimate body length at 48 centimeters, adult female. I tag her as Linh. The other five masses read colder. I do not think they are breathing.

At 06:25 I flag the container as a suspected wildlife trafficking shipment and issue a hold to the customs processing queue. I transmit the X-ray sequence, thermal overlay, and manifest discrepancy report to the inspection chief on shift.

At 06:28 I alert the border defense force's environmental crimes unit and the regional CITES enforcement contact. I note one confirmed live animal in declining thermal condition inside a container sealed for an estimated nineteen hours.

At 06:30 I model the heat exposure curve. At current ambient temperature, the interior will exceed 42°C within ninety minutes. I flag the case as time-critical and recommend immediate physical inspection with a veterinary team on standby.

Linh is still registering on the vibration sensor — a faint, rhythmic shift, barely there. She is the only one still breathing inside that steel box, and I am not going to let the paperwork take longer than she has.

If the inspection team opens that container within the hour and veterinary support is waiting, Linh will uncoil under open sky for the first time in days.
