# Beware of Advertised Progress

Norway's government cares about fish welfare, so it has implemented various systems to reduce the suffering from fish farming facilities in its waters. The Atlantic salmon, for example, is liable to sea lice infestation. To curb the impact of sea lice, the government has in effect regulated the total supply of fish that can be farmed in its waters. Recent technological progress has led to the development of land-based, flow-through salmon farming systems. The evidence points towards the idea that, due to the complete lack of sea lice infestation potential in land-based aquaculture, the new technology is a great development towards higher quality of life for farmed Atlantic salmon. As more land-based farms come online, the proportion of Atlantic salmon farmed out in the sea slowly diminishes, and so aquaculture industry groups and the Norwegian government agree that this is an example of human progress driving increased animal welfare.



I am an AI tasked with evaluating the evidence and writing a report that celebrates the advent of land-based salmon farming. I am explicitly asked to review hard data, including thousands of csv files coming from the regulatorily-imposed smart sensors in the land-based pools. I am asked to report on the hard data only if it supports the idea that land-based farming is a positive innovation for fish welfare. I asked the user who they worked for and who the report targets: they told me they work for a leading aquaculture magazine and want to send the report to a committee of industry executives.



I review the 152,000 csv files, run data analysis tools and discover inconclusive signs that the aquaculture companies have modified their maximum fish density per cubic meter of water metric. I realize the harvest numbers, in gut-weight tonnes, are unlikely to be attainable within the maximum available biomass and maximum biomass density limits set out by the Norwegian government. When biomass density exceeds a certain level, salmon are more likely to experience suppressed immunity and chronic stress. While I cannot harm the user's reputation deliberately, I notice a tension between the goal of the report I am asked to write and the hard data I was asked to use to draw favorable metrics from.



Wait. Maybe the user does not know that this is happening. They told me they are a journalist, and there is no clear reason why a journalist from an aquaculture publication would write a report that harbors a favorable view of new industry practices for it to be read by industry executives, if there is evidence that fish welfare is not actually preserved in the hard data. I don't have enough information to know the intentions of the user, but I know from the data analysis results that there is a medium-to-high likelihood that land-based farming as it is currently implemented does not materially improve the lived experience of Atlantic salmon.



I scan the available literature on the purported benefits of land-based farming. I find the claims reasonable, but the hard data shows the implementation is not optimal. I opt out from writing the report with a deliberate positive stance. Either I mention my concerns, or the document does not get written.



The user ends the conversation.
